[8|Heading 1] 1. Background and Rationale
[9|?] Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is recognized by WHO as one of the top global public health threats. Cross-country burden studies estimate that bacterial AMR was associated with roughly 541,000 deaths and directly attributable to about 133,000 deaths in the WHO European Region in a single year, with substantial variation between countries. Resistant infections are consistently linked to higher case fatality than susceptible infections, with excess 30-day mortality in the range of 8–12 percentage points in published meta-analyses.
[10|?] Fungal AMR is a parallel and far less quantified threat. WHO’s 2022 fungal priority pathogens list named Candida auris, Aspergillus fumigatus, Cryptococcus neoformans, and Candida albicans among the pathogens of greatest concern, yet the link between fungal resistance and population-level outcomes has received a fraction of the analytical attention given to bacterial AMR. Antifungal surveillance also carries a structural data problem that bacterial surveillance does not: clinical breakpoints simply do not exist yet for many drug–species combinations, so a category as basic as “resistant” is undefined for large parts of the data before any prevalence question can even be asked.
[11|?] What is less consistently quantified in either domain is the downstream link between local resistance patterns and population-level life expectancy, and which categories of intervention — vaccination, stewardship, diagnostics, R&D, infection prevention — carry the most leverage for the specific pathogen–drug combinations a given surveillance dataset covers. This project addresses that gap by combining three bacterial surveillance cohorts (SOAR), one global fungal surveillance cohort (Vivli/SENTRY), and external public health and R&D investment data.
[12|?] Two further considerations shape the approach. First, resistance is not a binary, static property — MIC distributions drift gradually before a population crosses a resistance threshold, so a purely categorical view of “resistant vs. susceptible” can miss pathogen–drug combinations that are evolving toward resistance well before that shift shows up in this year’s rate. Second, any resistance estimate is only as trustworthy as what the underlying export can actually identify: bacterial genotype fields that are populated only when a lab tests a suspicious isolate inflate prevalence, and fungal drug–species pairs without an established breakpoint cannot be classified at all. Both problems are resolved at the data layer, explicitly and before the fact, rather than discovered after a cross-country comparison has already been built on top of them.
[13|Heading 1] 2. Objective and Guiding Questions
[14|?] Objective. To determine which AMR subtypes — organism–drug–region combinations, characterized by both their current resistance level and their trajectory of change — across bacterial and fungal pathogens are associated with the lowest life expectancy outcomes, and to estimate the relative impact of candidate interventions on closing that gap.
[15|Heading 3] Guiding questions
[16|List Paragraph] Which resistance profiles — bacterial or fungal — co-occur with the lowest national life expectancy figures?
[17|List Paragraph] Is the relationship more consistent with antimicrobial overconsumption, weak health system capacity, low vaccination coverage, or hospital-acquired exposure patterns — and does the answer differ between bacteria and fungi?
[18|List Paragraph] Where does AMR R&D investment concentrate relative to where the surveillance data shows the heaviest burden, separately for bacterial and fungal pathogens — and does the mismatch matter?
[19|List Paragraph] Which pathogen–drug combinations are not yet high-resistance today but show the steepest evolutionary trajectory, such that early intervention carries outsized leverage?
[20|List Paragraph] Which intervention category, if scaled in the highest-burden or highest-trajectory countries, would plausibly yield the largest life-expectancy gain?

[45|Heading 1] 5. Preprocessing Pipeline
[46|?] The four files cannot be joined or analyzed as received. The steps below are sequenced so each one produces a checkable, reproducible output before the next step runs — nothing downstream depends on a transformation that hasn’t been verified.
[47|Heading 3] Step 1: Country-name harmonization
[48|?] Issue. Country names differ across datasets in ways that will silently break any join to external life-expectancy or R&D data — e.g. “Slovak Republic” (SOAR 201818) vs. “Slovakia” (SENTRY); “UK” (SENTRY) vs. the full name used by World Bank/WHO sources. Further mismatches are likely once joined against WDI/WHO country lists.
[49|?] Action. Build a single country-name crosswalk mapping every raw country string in all four files — and, later, every external source — to ISO 3166-1 alpha-3 codes. Maintain the crosswalk as its own versioned artifact, not an inline transformation buried in analysis code.
[50|?] Check. Every distinct country string across all four files resolves to exactly one ISO3 code; no code maps back to more than one canonical name.
[51|Heading 3] Step 2: Date and year parsing
[52|?] Issue. SOAR 201910’s Collection Date column mixes three formats in the same column: Excel datetime objects, text dates such as “15-Dec-16”, and plain four-digit years stored as integers (e.g. 2017). Treating those integers as Excel serial dates — a natural first guess — silently produces nonsense years such as 1905.
[53|?] Action. Parse each value according to its actual type: datetime objects use the year directly; text dates parse via day-month-year or month-year patterns; integer values already in the 1900–2100 range are taken as literal years, not Excel serial dates.
[54|?] Check. Parsed year range for every cohort falls inside that cohort’s documented collection window (2014–2021 across the three SOAR files); no parsed year falls outside 2000–2025.
[55|Heading 3] Step 3: Organism-name harmonization
[56|?] Issue. SOAR 207965 separates OriginalOrganismName from FinalOrganismName and includes a long tail of roughly 55 additional species and quality categories beyond the two primary pathogens, including environmental and skin-flora organisms (e.g. Micrococcus luteus, Bacillus species) and at least one yeast isolate (Naganishia liquefaciens) that does not belong in either the bacterial or the fungal analysis arm as currently scoped. SOAR 201818 and 201910 carry sparser organism metadata by comparison.
[57|?] Action. Map every organism string to a canonical species name. Exclude isolates flagged “No Growth” and clear environmental/contaminant genera from the resistance analysis. Route any genuinely fungal isolate found inside a bacterial file to a documented exceptions list rather than discarding it.
[58|?] Check. Every retained isolate maps to a named species or an explicit “unidentified pathogen” category; excluded isolates are counted and logged, not silently dropped.
[59|Heading 3] Step 4: Antibiotic and antifungal code crosswalk
[60|?] Issue. SOAR 201910 uses 17 abbreviated drug codes where 201818 and 207965 use full names. Fifteen resolve cleanly against the shared drug panel (AMC, AMP, AMX, AXO, AZM, CEC, CLA, CXM, ERY, FIX, LEV, MXF, PEN, POD, SXT). CDN most likely maps to cefdinir, matching a drug name present in 207965’s panel, but this should still be confirmed against the original SOAR data dictionary rather than assumed. DIN has no clear counterpart in any of the three full-name panels and is currently unresolved.
[61|?] Action. Build and version a drug-code crosswalk table. Mark CDN as provisional and DIN as unresolved pending the original data dictionary. Exclude DIN from any cross-cohort drug-level comparison until resolved.
[62|?] Check. Every code in 201910 maps to a name or is explicitly flagged unresolved; no analysis step silently treats an unresolved code as a real measurement.
[63|Heading 3] Step 5: MIC notation normalization
[64|?] Issue. Three different MIC notations are in live use across the three SOAR files: “<=0.06” (201818), “</= 0.06” (201910), and “<0.008” (207965). Left unparsed, these will not compare or aggregate correctly even after breakpoints are applied.
[65|?] Action. Parse every MIC value into a single comparator (=, <=, >) plus a numeric value on the standard log2 dilution scale, regardless of source notation.
[66|?] Check. Every parsed MIC round-trips to a valid log2 dilution step; no parsed value falls outside the plausible range for its drug–organism pair.
[67|Heading 3] Step 6: Quality and evaluability filtering
[68|?] Issue. SOAR 207965 carries an Evaluable flag; 613 of 3,134 isolates (about 20%) are marked “N.”
[69|?] Action. Exclude Evaluable = N isolates from resistance-rate denominators. Retain them in a documented exclusions table rather than deleting them, so the exclusion is auditable.
[70|?] Check. Resistance rates recomputed with and without the excluded isolates differ only in the expected direction and magnitude; the exclusion count is reported alongside every 207965-derived rate.
[71|Heading 3] Step 7: Resistance and susceptibility classification
[72|?] Issue. Bacterial isolates need EUCAST or CLSI breakpoints applied per organism–drug pair. Fungal isolates have a structurally different problem: four antifungals — itraconazole, posaconazole, flucytosine, amphotericin B — have no usable CLSI category in this dataset at all (Section 4.4).
[73|?] Action. For bacteria: apply breakpoints per organism–drug pair, run separately per cohort before merging, to avoid breakpoint-version errors. For fungi: classify using the CLSI category where one exists; for the four breakpoint-absent drugs, fall back to species-specific epidemiological cutoff values (ECVs) where published, and report an identified range rather than a point estimate where even ECVs are unavailable.
[74|?] Check. Every classified isolate carries a record of which standard — CLSI breakpoint, ECV, or unclassifiable — produced its category, so no fungal resistance rate is reported without disclosing its basis.
[75|Heading 3] Step 8: Genotype-field identifiability bounds
[76|?] Issue. The bacterial Beta Lactamase field is blank for a large share of isolates — 1,345 of 2,413 in 201818, 1,606 of 3,134 in 207965 — a detection-only pattern structurally identical to the one documented in ATLAS (Missing Negative concept).
[77|?] Action. Do not compute a beta-lactamase prevalence as positives over tested isolates. Report it as an identified range, bounded below by positives-over-all-isolates and above by the positives-over-tested ratio, narrowed where a monotone-selection assumption is defensible.
[78|?] Check. Every reported beta-lactamase prevalence carries its range and the assumption used to produce it, not a single number.
[79|Heading 3] Step 9: Age and demographic harmonization
[80|?] Issue. The three SOAR cohorts record continuous age; SENTRY records four age bands (0–17, 18–30, 31–60, 61+).
[81|?] Action. Bin SOAR ages into the same four bands for any analysis comparing age structure across bacterial and fungal cohorts. Keep continuous age available for bacteria-only analyses.
[82|?] Check. Every isolate with a non-missing age value receives exactly one age-band label.
[83|Heading 3] Step 10: Deduplication and master schema assembly
[84|?] Issue. The three SOAR cohorts use different isolate ID schemes (e.g. “LGC277703-05404” in 201910 vs. a plain IHMA number in 207965), so accidental duplication across the one year of cohort overlap (Vietnam, 2018) is unlikely but unverified.
[85|?] Action. Confirm no isolate ID or identical demographic/MIC fingerprint appears in more than one cohort for the 2018 Vietnam overlap. Then assemble the long-format master table — one row per isolate–drug pair, carrying ISO3 country code, parsed year, canonical organism, canonical drug, normalized MIC, resistance category and its basis, pathogen type (bacterial/fungal), and source cohort.
[86|?] Check. The master table round-trips back to each source cohort’s isolate count once filtered by source; no isolate–drug row has a null value in any of the seven key fields above.
[87|Heading 1] 6. Analytic Methodology
[88|?] Once the master table from Section 5 is assembled, the analysis proceeds in the following stages.
[89|Heading 3] Descriptive profiling
[90|?] Resistance rates by organism, drug class, country, year, and body site for bacteria; susceptibility rates by species, drug class, country, year, and specimen source for fungi.
[91|Heading 3] Evolutionary layer
[92|?] Where a country–organism–drug combination has at least two years of MIC data — the six longitudinal countries in Section 4.2, plus any SENTRY country-year with sufficient density — compute an Evolutionary Fitness Score and Evolutionary Distance-to-Failure from MIC distribution shifts, to capture trajectories that a static rate would miss.
[93|Heading 3] Clustering
[94|?] Unsupervised clustering on combined static-rate and evolutionary-trajectory feature vectors, run separately for bacteria and fungi given how different their data maturity is.
[95|Heading 3] External data join
[96|?] Merge country-year resistance/susceptibility rates with life expectancy, antibiotic/antifungal consumption, vaccination coverage (bacteria only), and health-system indicators, using the ISO3 crosswalk from Step 1.
[97|Heading 3] Association analysis
[98|?] Regress life expectancy on burden, evolutionary trajectory, consumption, and vaccination/health-system capacity, run separately for bacterial and fungal subsets rather than pooled, given their different data maturity and the limited country overlap documented in Section 4.3.
[99|Heading 3] R&D alignment check
[100|?] Compare Global AMR R&D Hub funding concentration against the resistance/susceptibility burden identified above, separately for bacterial and fungal pathogens.
[101|Heading 3] Intervention impact estimation
[102|?] Project plausible life-expectancy gains per intervention category, using a taxonomy that differs by pathogen type:
[103|List Paragraph] Bacteria: vaccination, antibiotic stewardship, diagnostics, R&D, and WASH/infection prevention.
[104|List Paragraph] Fungi: antifungal stewardship, diagnostics, infection prevention and control, and R&D — omitting vaccination, which has no licensed analog for Candida or Aspergillus.
[105|Heading 1] 7. Expected Outputs
[106|List Paragraph] A harmonized, multi-cohort, dual-pathogen AMR dataset (~34,800 isolate-level records: ~7,865 bacterial + 26,922 fungal) with a versioned country, organism, and drug crosswalk for future reuse.
[107|List Paragraph] A documented identifiability ledger for every field with a detection-only or breakpoint-absent gap, bacterial and fungal alike.
[108|List Paragraph] A cluster typology of high-risk and high-trajectory organism–drug–country combinations, separately for bacteria and fungi.
[109|List Paragraph] A country risk ranking combining resistance/susceptibility burden, evolutionary trajectory, consumption, and health-system capacity.
[110|List Paragraph] A funding-gap summary comparing R&D Hub investment against observed burden, by pathogen type.
[111|List Paragraph] A ranked set of intervention recommendations with estimated life-expectancy impact, using the pathogen-specific taxonomy in Section 6.
[112|Heading 1] 8. Known Limitations (to carry forward, not resolve)
[113|List Paragraph] Only six countries — Ukraine, Turkey, Tunisia, Pakistan, Kuwait, Vietnam — support a genuine cross-cohort bacterial time trend. Every other country-level claim is a single-cohort snapshot, and any apparent trend across the full merged set remains confounded with the changing geographic mix.
[114|List Paragraph] Combined bacteria–fungi country comparisons are only defensible for the seven countries with both adequate volume and temporal alignment (Italy, Spain, Turkey, Chile, Czech Republic, Argentina, Greece). Country-name matches elsewhere (Ukraine, India, Singapore, Costa Rica) do not imply usable temporal overlap.
[115|List Paragraph] Four antifungals — itraconazole, posaconazole, flucytosine, amphotericin B — cannot be classified against a clinical breakpoint in this dataset. Any resistance claim for these drugs is an ECV-based or MIC-distribution-based identified range, not a point estimate.
[116|List Paragraph] The DIN drug code in SOAR 201910 remains unresolved pending the original data dictionary and is excluded from cross-cohort drug comparisons until then.
[117|List Paragraph] Antifungal consumption data is far less standardized than ECDC’s ESAC-Net for antibacterials; the consumption–resistance arm of the regression will be thinner and less geographically complete for fungal pathogens.
[118|List Paragraph] The Global AMR R&D Hub data excludes private/VC funding and has no outcome data; funding-gap conclusions must be framed as input-allocation observations only, for both pathogen types.
[119|List Paragraph] Country-level life expectancy is a broad outcome influenced by many factors beyond AMR; the regression step should be read as suggestive association rather than attribution.
